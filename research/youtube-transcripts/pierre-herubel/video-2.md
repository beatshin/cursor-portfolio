Title: How I write LinkedIn posts' hooks that get 47,000+ views
Source: YouTube (speaker: Pierre Herubel)
Link: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2eovlLOHOU4

Cleaned Transcript:

The LinkedIn hook I am about to show you generated over 47,000 impressions.

I did not use an infographic, a long carousel, or spend hours writing. It was fast to create, and in this video I explain why it worked.

The hook:
"I create over 40 pieces of content per week. Here are my seven repurposing tactics."

This hook works because it follows three principles:
1. It grabs attention so people click "view more."
2. It targets the right audience.
3. It is specific enough to make the post topic immediately clear.

Why this specific hook performed well:

## 1) It starts from lived experience

Instead of saying "how to create 40 pieces of content per week," I say "I create over 40 pieces of content per week."

That creates intrigue because:
- The claim is concrete and bold.
- People may doubt it and want proof.
- People who want similar outcomes are motivated to read further.

The second sentence then explains the mechanism:
"Here are my seven repurposing tactics."

So the reader gets both:
- A result statement
- A clear path to how that result is achieved

## 2) It uses a hot topic and power words

"Repurposing" is a hot topic in my niche right now, so using it in the hook increases relevance and clicks.

When writing hooks, identify:
- Hot topics in your industry
- Power words that trigger emotion or curiosity

The objective is simple: increase "view more" clicks from the right people.

## 3) It uses numbers to increase specificity and actionability

The hook includes numbers in both lines:
- "40 pieces of content"
- "Seven repurposing tactics"

Numbers make the promise feel:
- Concrete
- Structured
- Easier to consume

There is a difference between "here are my repurposing tactics" and "here are my seven repurposing tactics." The second version creates stronger curiosity because the reader expects a complete, countable framework.

## 4) It delivers immediately after the hook

After the hook, I go straight into the list.

I avoid deceptive hooks and avoid long intros after "view more."

No extra setup, no fluff. The hook already introduces the context, so the body should go directly to the tactical value.

Example opening of the body:
"Tip #1: I use the extend repurposing process while everyone else is using the cut process."

## Results from this post

- 47,000+ impressions
- 547 reactions
- 153 comments (including mine)
- 32 reposts
- 40 sends
- 457 saves
- 203 profile visits
- 39 new followers

Key signal:
The number of saves is very high relative to reactions, which suggests the post was practical and worth revisiting.

The takeaway:
Strong hook + immediate actionable content = higher saves, stronger distribution, and more qualified profile activity.

## Summary

Pierre's framework for high-performing LinkedIn hooks is straightforward: lead with a concrete personal result, connect it to a relevant hot topic, and add numbers that make the promise specific and scannable. Then, once the reader clicks "view more," deliver value immediately with no padding. The strategy is designed for real feed behavior: capture attention fast, prove relevance, and reward the click with practical substance. The resulting performance pattern emphasizes saves and profile visits, not just vanity engagement.

## Application to LinkedIn for B2B SaaS

1. Build hooks from proof, not generic claims
- Use "we did X" instead of "how to do X."
- Quantify outcomes (for example: pipeline, activation, conversion lift).

2. Pair result + mechanism in two lines
- Line 1: Outcome statement.
- Line 2: Framework/list that explains how.

3. Use numbers intentionally
- Include a quantified result and a numbered structure.
- Make readers feel they will get a complete, usable breakdown.

4. Use niche-relevant hot topics
- Mirror active language in your buyer's current conversations.
- Avoid broad buzzwords with weak intent alignment.

5. Optimize for saves, not just likes
- Publish tactical posts people can reuse in meetings or workflows.
- Track save rate, profile visits, and follower quality.

6. Eliminate post-click friction
- After "view more," go directly into actionable steps.
- Remove long intros that delay value delivery.

7. Repeat as a system
- Create a weekly hook library by combining:
  - one real result,
  - one hot topic,
  - one numbered framework.

This turns hook writing from guesswork into a repeatable growth process.
