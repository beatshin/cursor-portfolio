Cleaned Transcript:

Welcome back to Mostly Growth, the number one podcast in product monetization.

Today we are discussing Sequoia's thesis: "Services as the new software." Their headline is that the next trillion-dollar company could be a software company masquerading as a services firm.

The core idea:
- If you sell a tool, you are in a race against the model.
- If you sell the work, model improvements make your service faster, cheaper, and harder to compete with.

Example:
- A company might spend around $10K/year on bookkeeping software.
- The same company might spend $120K/year on accountants.
- The bigger budget is labor, not software.

So the biggest winners may capture services budgets, not software budgets.

Why this matters for valuation:
- Investors seem to be pricing some AI-native legal/accounting companies as labor displacement plays.
- Traditional legal tech software trades at lower multiples because it sells software only.
- AI-native "service-with-software-under-the-hood" companies can go after a bigger pool of spend.

A key operational insight:
- Selling copilots into existing teams has a spending ceiling.
- Teams resist if they feel they are training their replacement.
- Selling an end-to-end outcome can be easier to buy and captures more value.

The value proposition becomes:
- Comparable quality to incumbent firms
- Lower cost
- Faster turnaround
- Better responsiveness

That is often more compelling than selling seats or features.

Real-world angle from accounting:
- Companies already pay significant monthly outsourced accounting fees.
- AI-native providers can bundle software + service.
- There is still a human judgment component, but less manual work.
- The target market is large because outsourced finance spend is large.

Where to build:
- Look for people-intensive businesses where labor is the dominant cost.
- Prioritize knowledge work done on computers.
- The more repetitive and process-driven the work, the more automation surface area exists.

Important caveat:
- This is not "zero humans."
- The winning model is often white-glove service on top, productized operations underneath.

In practice, AI can:
- Automate sourcing and monitoring tasks
- Generate outreach drafts
- Increase utilization of experts
- Reduce low-value repetitive work

That can improve service business economics by:
- Increasing throughput
- Smoothing capacity constraints
- Improving margin profiles
- Reducing dependency on linear headcount growth

Sector discussion:

1) Management consulting
- Likely not fully replaced by AI.
- The value is judgment, persuasion, stakeholder trust, and decision support.
- AI may reduce junior analyst workload, but senior judgment and sales remain core.

2) Call centers/customer support
- Highly exposed to disruption.
- AI can already resolve a large share of simpler cases.
- A hybrid model (AI + managed service) can outperform "buy software + hire separate support teams."

3) Legal services
- Contract review/drafting and routine legal ops are highly automatable.
- M&A and high-stakes negotiation remain judgment-heavy.
- Big near-term shift may be replacing or delaying in-house counsel hiring for routine workflows.

4) Accounting/finance
- Not "dead," but likely transformed.
- AI can create "10x accountant" workflows for teams that adopt it well.
- Service firms can offer better outcomes at lower prices while expanding margins.

Sequoia's framework discussed in the episode:
- Axis 1: outsourced vs insourced work
- Axis 2: intelligence-based vs judgment-based work

Most ripe for disruption:
- Outsourced + intelligence-heavy work
- Example areas: accounting/audit tasks, claims processing, payroll/compliance, real estate closing, KYC/AML

Less likely to be fully replaced:
- Work with high judgment, storytelling, trust management, or organizational influence

What moats might look like:
- Historical context and client-specific memory
- Embedded workflows and switching friction
- Relationship trust and brand credibility
- Niche specialization (industry-specific expertise)
- Faster time-to-value and superior service experience

Risk:
- Could become a race to the bottom on price if differentiation is weak.

Counterpoint:
- Service stickiness is real; if quality and reliability are strong, churn can remain low.
- AI can free capacity to invest more in client experience, not less.

Niche strategy opportunity:
- Hyper-specialized service firms can now scale in ways that were previously too expensive.
- "Local generalist" service models may lose share to "global niche expert" firms.

Valuation discussion:
- Classic software companies often command higher multiples because of growth narratives.
- Traditional services firms often trade on lower cash-flow multiples.
- AI-enabled service firms may sit between the two if they combine strong margins with scalable delivery.

Bull case:
- Service pricing power holds.
- Gross margins rise materially through automation.
- Investors award higher-than-traditional-services multiples due to software-like scalability.

Bear case:
- Services get commoditized.
- Differentiation fades.
- Multiples stay closer to traditional services.

Bottom line:
The opportunity is real, especially where expensive, repeatable, outsourced knowledge work exists. The winning model is likely not "software only" or "services only," but AI-enabled services that deliver clear outcomes with better economics.

## Summary

This episode argues that the biggest AI-native opportunities may come from selling outcomes rather than software seats. Instead of competing in crowded "copilot" markets, companies can capture larger labor budgets by delivering the full service with AI under the hood. The most attractive targets are outsourced, intelligence-heavy workflows where labor costs dominate and process repeatability is high. The hosts expect hybrid models (AI + expert humans) to win in the near term, with judgment-heavy domains remaining less disruptable than rules-based domains.

## Application for Operators

1. Pick the right wedge
- Start in workflows that are outsourced, repetitive, and labor-expensive.
- Avoid starting with domains where trust and bespoke judgment are the entire product.

2. Sell outcomes, not features
- Package around "we do X for you," not "we give you an AI tool."
- Price against service alternatives, not SaaS comparables.

3. Keep humans where judgment matters
- Use experts for escalation, interpretation, and relationship management.
- Use AI for intake, processing, drafting, QA, and workflow orchestration.

4. Build defensibility early
- Capture process memory, client context, and playbooks.
- Specialize in a high-value niche where domain depth is visible.

5. Track the right metrics
- Cost-to-serve
- Gross margin improvement
- Time-to-delivery
- Utilization of expert staff
- Retention/churn by cohort

6. Position for financing reality
- Assume investors may value you closer to services unless you prove repeatable, scalable margins.
- Show software-like operating leverage through data, not narrative alone.
